Principles of Enhancement (CIRTACI / SERAM 2024)

Vascular enhancement depends primarily on the iodine delivery rate (IDR, g I/s); parenchymal enhancement depends on the iodine load (g I/kg). These are the two key parameters to optimise depending on the study type.

IDR (g I/s) = Flow rate (ml/s) × Concentration (g/ml)
Iodine load (g I/kg) = Volume (ml) × Concentration (g/ml) / Weight (kg)
Volume (ml) = [Load (g/kg) × Weight (kg)] / Concentration (g/ml)
  • Higher IDR → earlier and higher vascular peak.
  • At equal total dose: higher concentration = lower volume + higher IDR = better arterial enhancement.
  • With poor venous access: increase concentration to maintain IDR at a lower flow rate.
  • Saline flush after contrast (30–40 ml): pushes the residual bolus, optimises opacification, reduces streak artifacts.
  • CM warmed to 37 °C: reduces viscosity ~25–30%; allows higher flow at lower injection pressure.

Enhancement Phases — Clinical Applications

Early arterial: 15–25 s
Late arterial: 35–45 s
Portal: 60–80 s
Nephrographic: 80–120 s
Equilibrium: 120–180 s
Delayed: 3–5 min
  • Early arterial (15–25 s): aortic CT angiography, pulmonary arteries (PE). IDR is the key parameter.
  • Late hepatic arterial (35–45 s): hypervascular lesions (HCC, hepatic adenoma, GIST/neuroendocrine metastases). Bolus threshold ~150 HU + 20–30 s delay.
  • Pancreatic phase (35–45 s): maximum differentiation from ductal adenocarcinoma (bright pancreas vs hypo-dense tumour).
  • Portal venous (60–80 s): peak hepatic enhancement (≥50 HU above baseline); detects hypovascular metastases; oncological staging.
  • Nephrographic (80–120 s): renal tumours, cortical–medullary differentiation.
  • Equilibrium (120–180 s): lesions with delayed enhancement (cholangiocarcinoma, HCC with washout).
  • Excretory (3–10 min): collecting system opacification; urothelial tumours, fistulae.

PE — Pulmonary Embolism (Optimised Protocol)

  • Pulmonary artery opacifies in 10–12 s → maximum effective injection time: 10 s.
  • Bolus tracking in main pulmonary trunk: threshold 80–100 HU, immediate trigger (no added delay).
  • The detector takes 4–7 s to begin acquisition → contrast arrives at the pulmonary trunk at scan start.
  • Proximal venous access (proximal forearm or antecubital fossa); 18G cannula when possible.
  • Simple breath-hold — NO Valsalva manoeuvre (Valsalva delays venous return and degrades the study).
  • Special patients:
    • Elderly (↓ cardiac output): use IDR 1.2–1.4 g I/s; peak is delayed but higher.
    • Athletes / pregnant (↑ cardiac output): increase IDR to 1.6–1.8 g I/s.
    • Obese (120–140 kV): IDR 1.6–2.0 g I/s to compensate for higher kilovoltage.
  • In moderate obesity (up to BMI 35): use Lean Body Weight (Janmahasatian); meta-analysis confirms equivalent diagnostic quality with significantly lower injected volume (Gulizia et al., EJR 2024).

Liver & Pancreas — Oncological Enhancement

  • Standard dose at 120 kV: 0.5 g I/kg (= 500 mg I/kg). Injection time 30 s.
  • Portal phase target: increase ≥ 50 HU above hepatic baseline; if direct-injection CT: liver ≥ 110 HU.
  • If hepatic steatosis: verify spleen ≥ 120 HU (spleen more reliable than liver as reference).
  • Fat tissue is poorly perfused → in obese patients, BSA-based dosing avoids overdose: Dose (g I) = BSA × 18.6 g I/m² (120 kV).
  • INNSZ Liver/Pancreas protocol: 580 mg I/kg · OW/OB-adjusted weight over 30 s.
  • kV reduction: at 100 kV use 0.4 g I/kg (or BSA × 15 g I/m²); at 80 kV: 0.3 g I/kg (normal weight only).
Dose (g I) = BSA (m²) × 18.6  →  120 kV
Dose (g I) = BSA (m²) × 15.0  →  100 kV
Dose (g I) = BSA (m²) × 11.0  →   80 kV (normal weight only)

Uro-CT — Split-Bolus Technique

  • 1st bolus (70% of dose): produces corticomedullary phase (25–40 s) and nephrographic phase (80–120 s); optimal detection of solid renal tumours.
  • 2nd bolus (30% of dose): injected immediately after the 1st; excreted later, opacifying the collecting system (excretory phase, 5–10 min).
  • A single delayed acquisition (5–10 min) simultaneously captures the nephrographic phase of the 2nd bolus + excretory phase of the 1st bolus.
  • Indications: unexplained haematuria, upper tract urothelial tumours, post-ureteral surgery assessment.
  • Additional delayed acquisition (10–15 min): useful in obstruction or asymmetric renal function.

Factors Affecting Enhancement

  • ↓ Cardiac output (heart failure, elderly): delayed and higher peak; increase acquisition delay; in PE ensure the bolus covers the entire pulmonary artery at the time of scanning.
  • ↑ Cardiac output (athletes, pregnancy, anaemia): earlier and lower peak; may require higher IDR.
  • Obesity: enlarged distribution volume; fat poorly perfused → TBW-based dosing overestimates. Use LBW (vascular) or BSA (parenchymal). BSA vs TBW reduces dose ~18% in obese patients.
  • kV reduction: brings the X-ray spectrum closer to the iodine K-edge (33.2 keV) → higher attenuation coefficient → same quality with less contrast. A 20 kV reduction ≈ 20% reduction in iodine dose and radiation.
  • GFR < 30 ml/min: weigh risk–benefit; pre-hydration; use minimum necessary dose.
  • CM temperature 37 °C: reduces viscosity ~25–30%; allows higher flow at lower injection pressure.

  • Reduce kV in 10% steps, validating quality criteria at each step before proceeding.
  • Combine with iterative reconstruction or AI-based reconstruction to compensate for increased noise.
  • Vascular IDR at 100 kV: 1.0–1.3 g I/s · at 80 kV: 0.8–1.0 g I/s.
